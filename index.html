Summary

ScenarioControl is the first vision-language control mechanism for learned driving scenario generation. Given a text prompt or an input image, it synthesizes diverse, realistic 3D scenario rollouts — including map structure, reactive agents over time, pedestrians, infrastructure, and ego-view observations. The model generates scenes in a vectorized latent space that jointly represents road structure and dynamic agents. With this, it can produce temporally consistent scenario rollouts from the perspective different actors in the scene, as well as long-horizon continuation of driving scenarios.

To connect multimodal control with sparse vectorized scene elements, we introduce a cross-global control mechanism that combines standard cross-attention with a lightweight global-context branch, enabling fine-grained control over road layout and traffic conditions while preserving realism. Extensive experiments validate that the control adherence and fidelity of ScenarioControl is favorable to all tested methods across all experiments. To facilitate further training and evaluation, we release a dataset with text annotations aligned to vectorized map structures and visual context.

Method

ScenarioControl models each scene as a graph of objects and lane centerlines within a 64×64 m bird's-eye view, augmented with per-object elevation so generated scenarios can be faithfully reprojected into the image domain. A controllable latent diffusion model — conditioned on text prompts or agent-view images — produces this vectorized scene representation in a single forward pass. To fuse dense conditioning signals with sparse scene tokens, we introduce a gated cross-global control mechanism that combines standard cross-attention with a compact global-context branch, while a separate count-injection head provides explicit control over lane and agent cardinality. Together these components enable fine-grained edits to road layout and traffic composition without sacrificing topological or geometric consistency.

The vectorized scene representation supports existing BEV-space behavior simulation, which we project back into the camera frame to obtain wireframe control signals for video generation. These signals condition a LoRA-adapted Wan 2.2 (5B) video model, which we train in two variants for additional appearance control: an image-conditioned model that uses the first frame for appearance, and a prompt-conditioned model whose appearance is set by a text description. Because video generation is grounded in the vectorized scenario, we can re-render the wireframe from arbitrary camera extrinsics and synthesize coherent rollouts from the perspectives of agents other than the ego vehicle, supporting both video continuation and entirely novel traffic situations.

Large Scene Generation

Although our diffusion model is trained on local 64×64 m scenes, we extend it to much larger environments via iterative outpainting. Starting from an image- or prompt-conditioned initial scene, we repeatedly grow the layout outward, producing diverse multi-block road networks: distinct intersection types, lane configurations, and agent distributions that remain coherent in topology end-to-end. Comparison to Baselines

We compare our cross-global control mechanism against concatenation — directly concatenating conditioning features with the scene tokens, a common fallback for fusing dense control signals into sparse token sequences. Across both image- and prompt-conditioned settings, our method delivers substantially better adherence to the conditioning while preserving scene realism: in the image-conditioned setting alone, it reduces collisions by 43.4% and improves global control (Control Sensitivity Correlation) by 52.8% relative to concatenation.
